Neopronouns

What are neopronouns

  • Neopronouns are a category of neologistic English third-person personal pronouns beyond 'she', 'he', 'they', 'one' and 'it
  • Neologistic: A new word, expression, or usage

Why are neopronouns used

  • A neopronoun can be a word a created to serve as pronouns without expressing gender
  • Neopronouns are used when an individual who feels that their gender goes beyond male, female or non-binary

Who uses neopronouns

  • Neopronouns can be used by anyone, though most often they are used by transgender, non-binary, and/or gender nonconforming people. Like all pronouns, neopronouns are personal to each individual who uses them, but they all share one thing in common: people who use neopronouns do so to feel comfortable and have their humanity acknowledged and respected.

Some examples of neopronouns

  • ey/em
  • fae/faer
  • e/em
  • ze/hir
  • xe/xem/xyr
  • ze/zir/zir/zirs/zirself
  • ae/aer/aer/aers/aerself
  • ve/ver/vis/vis/verself
  • ne/nem/nir/nirs/nemself

Pronouncing neopronouns

  • ey/em/eir - pronounced "ay/em/heir"
  • fae/faer/faers - pronounced "fay/fair/fairs"
  • xe/xem/xyr - pronounced "zee/zem/zeer"
  • ze/hir/hirs - pronouned "zee/heer/heers"

Neopronouns in a sentence

  • xe/xem/xyr: "Xe doesn't want ketchup with xyr fries"
  • ze/hir/hirs: "Ze doesn't want ketchup with hir fries"
  • fae/faer/faers : "Fae doesn't want ketchup with faer fries"s
    • ey/em/eir: "Ey doesn't want ketchup with eir fries"

Why we should respect neopronouns?

  • Respecting neopronouns helps others feel seen ad validated
  • Respecting Neopronouns helps normalize them
  • They are most often used by neurodivergent people who feel a disconnect from traditional genders

Neopronouns History

  • E/Em/Eir/Eirs/Emself: This set of pronouns is often referred to as the Spivak pronouns, which began being used in the 1990s after Michael Spivak used them in his manual so that no person would have a specified gender
  • Co/Co/Cos/Cos/Coself: These pronouns were created by Mary Orovan in 1970 to be a pronoun option in place of he or she.
  • Thon/Thon/Thons/Thons/Thonself: This set of pronouns as recorded by Merriam-Webster as a conjugation of “That one” which became Thon.
