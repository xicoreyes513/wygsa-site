Ballroom Culture

What is Ballroom?

  • Ball culture, drag ball culture, the house-ballroom community, the ballroom scene or ballroom culture and similar terms describe a young African-American and Latin American underground LGBTQ+ subculture that originated in New York City, in which people "walk" for trophies, prizes, and glory at events known as balls.

Who started it? Where?

  • In the early 1970s, Harlem drag queen Lottie LaBeija convinced Crystal LaBeija to promote her own ball. Crystal agreed, and the House of LaBeija—the first ever ballroom “house”—was born, with Crystal at the helm as the “mother.”
  • Crystal and Lottie went on to host the first house ball in Harlem in the early 1970s, entitling it “Crystal & Lottie LaBeija presents the first annual House of LaBeija Ball.” The ball, designed exclusively for Black and Latinx trans, gay and queer people, was a success.

How were Houses created?

  • Kids who were left homeless after being kicked out by homophobic or transphobic relatives, formed their own families, known as houses. These houses were named after the head, or “mother” of the house — an elder queer person who was often not much older than their “children.” They all lived and competed as a family to bring glory, in the form of trophies, to their houses, earning the title of “legendary”.

Competition

  • To compete against each other, Houses "walk" in the balls judged on vogue skills, costumes, appearance, and attitude. Participants dress according to the category in which they are competing, and are expected to display appropriate "realness".The largest balls last as long as ten hours, with dozens of categories in a single evening
  • With fewer spectators, nearly everyone comes to compete; some trophies are 12 feet tall, and a grand-prize winner can earn $1,000 or more.

Categories

  • Butch Queen (BQ) Realness - Judged on participants' ability to blend in with male heterosexuals.
  • Femme Queen (FQ) Realness - Judged on participants' ability to blend in with cisgender women.
  • Runway - Judged on participants' ability to walk like a supermodel. Broken up into different categories.
  • BQ/FQ/FF Face - Judging a participant's facial structure, skin, and teeth.
  • Hands Performance - Using the hands to tell a story.
  • Beginner's Vogue - Vogue category for participants who have been voguing for less than two years.
  • Best Dressed - Judged on a participant's ostentatious clothing.
  • Women's Vogue - Women who vogue.
  • Labels - Judged on the labels a participant is wearing, their authenticity and the overall look.
  • Butch Queen Vogue Fem/Female Figure Performance - Use the vogue elements of hands, catwalk, duckwalk, floor performance, spins and dips.

Cities with prominent ball Culture

  • New York City
    • New York City is the epicenter of the world's drag ball culture. Crystal LaBeija and Lottie began their own drag ball titled 'House of LaBeija,' kickstarting the current ballroom scene in New York
  • Washington, DC
    • The Washington ballroom scene was created in the mid to late 1980s by Icon Lowell Adonis Khanh, Icon Eric Christian-Bazaar, and Icon Mother Juan Aviance. During the 1990s, more houses appeared in the area due to the efforts of Twain Miyake-Mugler, Icon Harold Balenciaga, Icons Shannon Garcon and Whitney Garcon. The city hosts a series of annual balls, in which contestants compete for trophies and cash prizes.
  • Baltimore
    • In 1931, the newspaper Baltimore Afro-American covered a local drag ball. By the 1930s, the drag ball culture was starting to merge in the Black communities in major cities such as Baltimore, Chicago, and New York.
  • Philadelphia
    • Philadelphia's first ball was the Oynx Ball which took place in August 1989.The documentary How Do I Look partially focused on the ball community in Philadelphia
  • Atlanta
    • Atlanta has the most prominent ball community south of Washington, D.C. Several balls are held in Atlanta each year. Several major houses established in other major cities have opened chapters in Atlanta
